Understanding Heatwave Prediction through Data Assimilation

Initial Condition Sensitivity EnKF Observation Density Heatwave Forecasting

Introduction & Goal

Heatwaves are among the most impactful extreme weather events. Accurate prediction is important for public safety, energy demand, and climate risk management. However, numerical weather prediction is highly sensitive to initial conditions, meaning small errors in the initial atmospheric state can grow into large forecast errors.

Background

Data Assimilation Concept

Data assimilation combines model background information with observations to produce a more accurate atmospheric state called the analysis.

analysis = background + Kalman gain × innovation

In this project, ERA5 is treated as the background field, ASOS is used as observation, and the difference between observation and background is called innovation.

Key Definitions

Background: ERA5 model estimate

Observation: ASOS measured temperature

Innovation: observation − background

Analysis: DA-corrected temperature

Method

Data Sources

ASOS surface temperature observations

Matrix-based Data Assimilation

To better connect with the course formulation, we applied a matrix-based DA approach using the Kalman gain equation. The background error covariance was approximated using a distance-based Gaussian model.

K = PbHT(HPbHT + R)-1

This is still a simplified framework, but it preserves the core DA structure: background, observation, innovation, Kalman gain, and analysis update.

Spatial Analysis: SE vs GW OSE Experiment

The spatial comparison experiment evaluates how observation density and regional characteristics influence DA performance. SE and GW were treated as separate observing systems.

RMSE Comparison

Case Background RMSE Analysis RMSE RMSE Reduction Improvement Mean Increment Mean Abs. Increment

Interpretation

  • DA reduced RMSE by about 24% in both SE and GW.
  • GW showed larger absolute correction, suggesting larger background model error.
  • SE had negative increments, indicating ERA5 overestimation.
  • GW had positive increments, indicating ERA5 underestimation.

Conclusion

  • Data assimilation improved temperature estimates in both regions, but GW required stronger corrections due to larger background errors.
  • DA reduced RMSE in both time and spatial comparison experiments.
  • Observation density and regional characteristics affected correction magnitude.
  • Gangwon showed larger correction, likely due to sparse observations and complex terrain.
  • The experiment demonstrates the practical role of DA in heatwave prediction.
